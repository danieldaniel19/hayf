# Training Doctrine

Evidence-first shared training model for all fitness specialists.

## Adaptation

- Training creates a stress signal; useful adaptation requires adequate recovery and repeated exposure.
- Specificity matters: the body adapts most strongly to the tissues, energy systems, movement patterns, and intensities it practices.
- Overload should be small, legible, and earned. Progress duration, frequency, intensity, density, or complexity, not all at once.
- Consistency is an adaptation input. A repeatable minimum week is more valuable than an ambitious week that collapses.

## Energy Systems

- Aerobic work supports durability, recovery between efforts, and repeatable low-to-moderate output.
- Threshold work improves sustained hard output but carries meaningful fatigue cost.
- VO2max work targets high oxygen uptake and high-end aerobic power; it is potent and should be dosed sparingly.
- Anaerobic and sprint work improve short high-power output, coordination, and recruitment, with high neuromuscular cost.
- Strength work improves force capacity, tissue tolerance, movement economy, and body-composition support.

## Training Tools

- Long easy sessions build aerobic durability with lower acute intensity cost.
- Tempo and steady work bridge easy aerobic work and threshold-specific training.
- Threshold intervals should be controlled and repeatable, not all-out.
- VO2 intervals should protect quality and recovery; one weekly exposure is often enough for mixed goals.
- Sprints and neuromuscular work require freshness and careful spacing.
- Recovery sessions must reduce total stress, not disguise extra volume.

## Planning

- Weekly structure should protect high-value sessions and make recovery visible.
- Hard days need spacing. Lower-body strength, hard cycling, and hard running compete for overlapping fatigue budgets.
- Deloads or lower-dose weeks are appropriate when adherence drops, recovery worsens, or load has increased for several weeks.
- Plans should distinguish committed near-term work from draft future work.

## Fatigue

- Cardiorespiratory fatigue: breathlessness, elevated easy-session effort, poor repeatability.
- Muscular fatigue: soreness, local heaviness, reduced force production.
- Neuromuscular fatigue: poor coordination, reduced snap, low readiness for power work.
- Connective tissue risk: joint/tendon irritation, impact sensitivity, pain that worsens with loading.
