# Product Positioning

## HAYF in one sentence

HAYF, short for "How Are You Feeling," is a personal training coach for busy, tech-forward people balancing strength and cardio who want to know what to do today without guesswork.

The final product vision is a holistic coaching experience across fitness, nutrition, and mind. V1 focuses on fitness, but the product should be positioned and designed so nutrition and mind can become first-class coaching domains later.

## Primary ICP

HAYF is not for elite athletes chasing marginal gains first.

It is for:

- busy professionals with limited planning time
- tech workers and early AI adopters who already use ChatGPT or similar tools for fitness planning
- ambitious but inconsistent exercisers who want help staying on track
- people trying to balance strength and endurance without manually researching or rebuilding a plan every week

Typical user profile:

- has some training experience
- mixes gym work with running, cycling, conditioning, or other cardio
- values competence and progress but does not want a complicated planning hobby
- trusts software to help make decisions if it feels intelligent and personal

## Core problem

Users know they should balance strength and cardio, but real life keeps disrupting execution:

- work compresses the day
- recovery changes
- motivation fluctuates
- travel interrupts routine
- available time shrinks
- context changes after the plan was made

Existing AI tools help, but they lack the right memory, continuity, and structured context to coach well across time.

## Product promise

HAYF should make the user feel:

"I know what to train today based on my data and intelligent guidance. I am not guessing. And if something changes, I can talk to the app and it will adapt without compromising my long-term goals."

That promise has four parts:

- decide today's training clearly
- balance strength and cardio across the week
- adapt in the moment when reality changes
- preserve long-term consistency instead of reacting blindly

Long term, that promise expands from "what should I train today?" to "what should I do for my body and mind today?" Fitness remains the first wedge, but nutrition and mind should eventually inform the same coaching relationship.

## Product evolution

HAYF should evolve in deliberate layers:

- V1: fitness coaching for strength/cardio balance, workout recommendations, check-ins, HealthKit context, and workout follow-through
- V2: nutrition coaching that understands training goals, recovery needs, preferences, and real-life constraints
- V3: mind coaching that understands stress, motivation, confidence, consistency, and habit formation
- Final vision: fitness, nutrition, and mind coaches are aware of one another, can exchange advice, and can take bounded user-approved actions

The agentic direction matters, but it should not bloat v1. Early product and technical decisions should create clean domain boundaries, shared context objects, and an action layer that can later support tasks such as adding a workout to the user's calendar.

## Product posture

The differentiator is the personal coach experience, enabled by AI but not branded as "AI for AI's sake."

AI should feel:

- clearly intelligent and adaptive
- present throughout the journey
- easy to access through chat at any moment
- subtle in branding and tone

The selling point is not "AI."
The selling point is "a coach that understands the user's context and adapts with them."

## UX implications

The product should support both structured input and conversational adaptation.

Important product tension:

- avoid forcing every action through chat
- preserve efficient UX-driven inputs for speed and clarity
- still make the coach presence obvious and available

Representative moments to design for:

- the user lands after a 12-hour flight and wants to stay on track on vacation
- the user only has 30 minutes instead of the planned 45 after work
- the user is anxious and wants to swap a planned strength session for a run
- the user needs route ideas or a more motivating alternative to get moving

Future context sources that strengthen this coach model:

- location
- weather
- calendar
- nutrition guidance
- mind and stress context
- cross-domain coach recommendations

## Brand and design direction

HAYF should not look like a generic fitness app.

Desired feel:

- highly personal
- calm
- coach-like
- intelligent
- minimal
- functional before aesthetic
- somewhere between elite and everyday

It should not feel like:

- a macho performance brand
- a colorful startup wellness app
- a biohacker dashboard
- a hardcore endurance app
- a soft mental health app
- a generic Apple-clone minimal app with no point of view

## Visual direction

Lean toward an almost brutal monochrome system:

- whites
- greys
- black
- a very restrained HAYF orange accent

Also worth testing:

- cool whites with graphite and black plus the same HAYF orange accent

Hybrid-athlete positioning should be visually explicit:

- strength
- endurance
- cardio

But the product world should still feel restrained, premium, and systematic rather than visually busy.
